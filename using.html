How to use this notebook

This is a notebook, not a curriculum. The pages don't need to be visited in order. There is no quiz at the end. What follows is the sequence that works for most of you the first time through. It mirrors the cognitive arc the assessment expects: find a question, hear more than one answer to it, evaluate those answers, defend one of your own.

Step One

You are here.

Look in the navigation: welcome

The welcome page is the front of the notebook — a single question to make sure you remember what philosophy actually does to a mind. Read it, then click Begin. You'll land on the task.

Step Two

Read what the task actually is.

Look in the navigation: the task

Five short principles, then an A-grade and a C-grade extract side by side. The A response is annotated with the moves it makes that the C response doesn't. You will not earn the A by accident. Read this page once, properly. Most of the things students lose marks on are visible in the C extract.

At the end of the task you'll see a legend for the four design criteria — KU / R / CA / C (Knowledge & Understanding, Reasoning, Critical Analysis, Communication). These reappear in the drafting scaffold so you always know which criterion each section of your work is building.

Step Three

Find a question that catches you.

Look in the navigation: questions

117 philosophical questions, every one phrased so that taking a position is unavoidable. The chips along the top filter by key area (ethics / epistemology / metaphysics) and by difficulty (entry / standard / ambitious). The search box accepts keywords and philosopher names. Type Murdoch and only the questions in her territory remain.

Click any question to expand it. You'll see the positions you'll need to engage, the counter-positions you'll need to anticipate, and the readings that support the question. If a question lights you up, click Pin this. Pinned questions follow you into the chamber and the drafting scaffold.

Pin three or four. Picking is part of the assessment; you don't need to commit on the first pass.

Step Four

Sharpen the question in the chamber.

Look in the navigation: chamber

The chamber is the part of the site that's unfamiliar, and the part students misuse most. It is a Socratic interlocutor — an AI that has been instructed never to write your essay, never to give you a complete question, and never to summarise the readings for you. It will only ask you questions. That is on purpose.

Pick the topic chip that matches your question (or leave auto-detect on for general questions). The chip tells the agent which set of readings to keep in mind: Murdoch and Nussbaum if you choose virtue / compassion, Sartre and Camus if you choose existentialism, and so on.

Then type your half-formed thought into the input on the right. Some patterns that work:

  • Give the agent something to push against. "I think we're not really free; here's why…" works better than "What does Sartre think about freedom?"
  • When the agent asks you to convert a topic into a question, do it. The SACE moderators say this is the single most impactful move you can make.
  • When you name one position, the agent will ask for the strongest objection to it. Take that prompt seriously. Steelmanning the other side is the move that most distinguishes A from C.
  • If you ask the agent to write a paragraph for you, it will refuse warmly. That is the assessment refusing — not a malfunction.
  • If you ask "what does Sartre think?", the agent will point you to the reading rather than summarising. Read it, then come back with a position to test.
Step Five

Read against the canon.

Look in the navigation: readings

The readings library lists every text loaded into your unit, organised by topic. Each card gives you the author, the title, and a one-paragraph orientation telling you what argument the reading makes and why it matters. Click Discuss in chamber on any card to land in the chamber with that reading already in scope.

You should engage two to four readings deeply, not survey twenty. The Issues Study rewards depth over breadth: naming a philosopher, presenting an objection, presenting a reply, taking a position with reasons.

Step Six

Draft, in five sections.

Look in the navigation: drafting

A scaffold that mirrors the structure the assessment is checking for: your question, the positions on it, a critical analysis of those positions, your defended position, your sources. Each section is labelled with the design criteria it builds (KU1 / KU2+R1 / CA1+R2 / R1+R2 / C2).

Type directly into each section. The word counter in the right rail climbs toward 800. That is the maximum, not the target; a tighter argument always beats a longer one. The scaffold auto-saves to your browser as you type. When you're ready to draft the actual piece in your word processor, hit Export as Markdown.

Where to find what

  • The four design criteria (KU / R / CA / C) Each section of drafting is labelled with the criteria it builds. The full legend lives at the bottom of the task.
  • The A-vs-C exemplar comparison Second half of the task.
  • A specific philosopher (Murdoch, Sartre, Haidt…) The search box on questions, or browse readings by topic.
  • Your pinned questions They follow you everywhere. The right rail of questions, chamber, and drafting.
  • Word count Right rail of drafting.

Notes in the margin

A note from the editor The order is a guide, not a track. If you already have a question, jump to the chamber. If you've already read Sartre, skip the bank.
About the agent It will never write your essay. If you find yourself trying to extract paragraphs from it, stop and ask: who is this assessment for?
Common pitfall You'll pin questions you don't end up using. That isn't waste. Picking is the assessment.
If the chamber feels generic Give it more to work with: a half-formed claim, a passage you're stuck on, a position you're trying to argue against. The agent works at the level of what you give it.
From SACE moderators Phrase your topic as a question. Not "Free will" — but "If neuroscience can predict our choices before we make them, do we still have free will?"
